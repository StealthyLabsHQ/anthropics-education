# In-Depth Report: AI Detection in Compilatio Studium, Magister and Magister+

> **When to load this file:** when a student asks specifically about Compilatio,
> wants to understand how institutional detectors work, or needs to calibrate
> their humanization effort against a known commercial pipeline.

---

## 1. Detection Architecture: LLM and Technologies Used

### The Detection Engine

Compilatio does not disclose the name of its proprietary LLM, but its documentation
describes a **specialized Natural Language Processing (NLP) AI** built on an
in-house Large Language Model. This model was trained on a mixed corpus: human
texts on one side, AI-generated texts on the other. The approach is that of a
**machine learning classifier** (pattern recognition) that identifies stylistic,
statistical, and linguistic features differentiating the two sources.

### AI Models Targeted on Input

The official page `compilatio.net/ia-detecteur-info` explicitly lists the tools
detected (as of 2 April 2026):

- **OpenAI**: GPT-5.4, GPT-5.4 Pro, GPT-5.2, GPT-5.1, GPT-5, GPT-4.5, GPT-4o, GPT-4o Mini
- **Anthropic**: Claude Sonnet 4.6, Claude Opus 4.6, Claude Sonnet 4.5
- **Google**: Gemini 3.1 Pro, Gemini 3.1 Pro Thinking, Gemini 2.5 Flash, Gemini 3 Pro, Copilot
- **Mistral** (French model)
- **Meta**: Llama
- **Others**: DeepSeek, Jasper, YouChat, and "many others"

The detection system version in service at the time of writing (per official
documentation) is **v4.5.3** (deployed 4 September 2025). The previous documented
version was **v4.2.1** (in service since October 2024).

### Five-Step Detection Method (officially described)

1. **Text segmentation**: identification of stylistically homogeneous passages
2. **Labelling**: AI or human classification for each passage
3. **Grouping**: aggregation of AI passages into "points of interest"
4. **Cleaning**: filtering of irrelevant passages to reduce false positives
5. **Presentation**: display of the final score in the report

The two key linguistic metrics are **perplexity** (text predictability — AI texts
are less surprising) and **burstiness** (human writing mixes short and long sentences;
AI texts are more uniform).

## 2. Metrics and Detection Thresholds

### The AI Score: a Continuous Percentage, No Official Published Threshold

The AI detection score is a **percentage between 0% and 100%** indicating the
proportion of text whose style resembles AI output. Compilatio **publishes no
official alert threshold**: there is no cut-off of the type "above X% the document
is automatically flagged." The score is presented as a probabilistic indicator
to be interpreted contextually.

The wording chosen is deliberately cautious: **"texts potentially generated by AI"**,
not "texts generated by AI." For a 1,000-word document with a 10% score, this means
100 words display a style similar to AI output.

### Published Reliability Metrics (v4.5.3)

| Indicator | Value | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Precision | 99% | Of 100 passages flagged as "AI", 99 truly are |
| Recall | 98% | Of 100 AI passages, 98 are correctly detected |
| Accuracy | 99% | Of 100 passages (human + AI), 99 correctly labelled |
| False positive rate | < 1% | Fewer than 1 human text in 100 wrongly flagged |

These measurements were carried out on **~7,400 texts in 24 languages**, with a
balanced corpus of 3,700 human texts and 3,700 AI texts generated from simple
prompts (no specific style instructions). The overall system accuracy for
heterogeneous (mixed) documents is announced at **9/10** (9 passages in 10
correctly labelled).

### Inter-Analysis Variability

Compilatio officially acknowledges that scores can vary for the same document
between analyses, for three reasons: the probabilistic nature of the model,
encoding differences depending on file format (PDF vs DOCX), and **regular
algorithm adjustments**.

## 3. Technical Differences Between Studium, Magister and Magister+

### Official Comparison Table (Compilatio documentation, 2026)

| Feature | Magister | Magister+ | Studium |
|---|---|---|---|
| Exact similarity and light paraphrase detection (syntactic) | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ |
| **AI content detection** | ❌ | ✅ | ✅ |
| Deep paraphrase detection (semantic) | ❌ | ✅ | ❌ |
| Multilingual similarity / translation detection | ❌ | ✅ | ❌ |
| Altered text detection (bypass attempts) | ❌ | ✅ | ❌ |
| Grammar & spell checker | ❌ | ✅ | ❌ |
| Priority processing | ❌ | ✅ | N/A |
| Comparison with global Magister corpus | ✅ | ✅ | ❌ |
| AI score included in global suspect text % | ❌ | ✅ | partial |

**Target audience**: Magister and Magister+ are aimed at **teachers/institutions**.
Studium is the **student** tool (self-check). AI detection in Studium provides a
score but without the level of detail available in the Magister+ report.

### What Specifically Distinguishes Magister+

Magister+ is the premium version that adds on top of Magister:

**AI detection** (absent from base Magister): score for content potentially
generated by AI, with highlighted passage localisation in the document.

**Semantic detection** (deep paraphrases): Magister+ identifies passages that are
similar *in meaning*, even when the words are entirely different. This feature
relies on a vector representation of content (embeddings) and compares semantic
proximity between passages. It is controlled by the institution administrator
(activatable/deactivatable). A **minimum number of shared words** is required to
trigger analysis; extreme paraphrases with no shared vocabulary may escape detection.

**Altered text detection**: identifies deliberate alteration attempts (e.g. Unicode
character substitutions, invisible character insertions) designed to conceal
plagiarism or AI use.

**Multilingual side-by-side view**: allows viewing the source in its original language.

## 4. False Positive Rates: Empirical Data and Academic Studies

### Official Compilatio Data

Compilatio claims a false positive rate of < 1% **under its own test conditions**,
with a corpus of human texts "representative of student work." The company itself
underlines that these metrics are conditional: the prompts used to generate AI
texts were simple, with no style instructions. Performance could differ with
stylised or post-edited AI texts.

### Key Independent Study: Dessus & Seyve (2024/2025)

The only independent French academic study available on Compilatio Magister+ is
that of **Philippe Dessus and Daniel Seyve** (Université Grenoble Alpes), published as:
- HAL preprint (2024): *"Le cas de Compilatio Magister+"*, hal-04578682
- Article in **STICEF** (Sciences et Technologies de l'Information et de la
  Communication pour l'Éducation et la Formation), Vol. 32, No 1, 2025, pp. 112–128

**Main results from available abstracts**:
- The study tested Compilatio Magister+ on **86 documents** (academic texts in a
  university context)
- For detection of human texts: **Compilatio Magister+ achieved error-free scores —
  zero false positives** in this sample
- Among other detectors tested comparatively, only one third-party study (Bellini
  et al., 2024) showed good performance: **13 true positives for 4 false positives**;
  other detectors showed significantly weaker results

This study is notable because it constitutes a test **under real conditions** with
French-speaking students on academic texts — a substantially harder terrain than
Compilatio's internal benchmarks.

### Important Nuances on False Positives

Compilatio officially acknowledges several high-risk situations for false positives:

- **Technical or formal academic texts**: their conventional structure and stylistic
  uniformity can be confused with AI style
- **Short texts**: insufficient linguistic data for reliable analysis
- **Mixed texts** (co-written by human + AI then corrected)
- **Highly formal writing styles**: a human whose style resembles AI output may be
  wrongly flagged

Feedback from teachers on Campus Matin (December 2023) reported reliability "around
90%" with "10% possibility of a false positive" — corresponding to broader
institutional usage conditions than the optimal test conditions.

## 5. Documented Bypass Vectors

### What Compilatio Itself Acknowledges

**AI-corrected texts (not paraphrased)**: if a student asks ChatGPT to correct
only spelling mistakes, the underlying structure remains the student's own, without
AI markers. The tool objectively considers the text human. This is a **documented
blind spot**.

**Lightly paraphrased texts**: depending on the degree of paraphrasing, the text
gradually moves away from AI markers. The deeper the paraphrase, the more uncertain
detection becomes.

**Very short texts**: insufficient data for reliable analysis.

**Intensive post-editing**: thorough manual editing can erase AI "fingerprints."

### Vectors Identified in the Literature and Community

Several bypass mechanisms are documented in the community (notably on Reddit,
TikTok, and dedicated platforms), though academic research specific to Compilatio
remains limited:

**Text humanisers**: tools branded "AI humaniser" (often paraphrasers trained to
imitate human style) are actively searched on French student forums in 2025–2026 —
indirect evidence of the initial effectiveness of detection.

**Injection of personal elements**: personal experiences, anecdotes, and deliberate
stylistic imprecision (which increases perplexity) reduce the AI score.

**Deep multi-step paraphrasing**: asking an AI to paraphrase several times with
varying styles, or using multiple AI models successively, can attenuate
model-specific stylistic markers.

**Unicode alterations**: Compilatio declares having a "detection of altered texts"
module specifically to counter the insertion of visually identical Unicode characters
(e.g. Cyrillic or Greek characters resembling Latin ones) — confirming this vector
had been documented and used.

**General structural limitations of AI detectors** (applicable to Compilatio): the
arms race with constantly evolving AI models is a permanent challenge. Models like
GPT-5 produce texts increasingly close to human writing, forcing regular retraining
of the detector.

## 7. Critical Synthesis

**What Compilatio does well**: the system is well documented, multilingual (24 to
35+ languages), integrated into a complete pedagogical pipeline, and the independent
Dessus & Seyve study (2025) partially validates the claimed performance (zero false
positives on 86 human texts). The cautious presentation of results as "potentially
generated by AI" and the insistence on the teacher as final judge are pedagogically
responsible.

**Limitations to know**: internal test conditions (homogeneous texts, simple prompts)
are more favourable than real-world conditions with complex academic texts. The
absence of an official threshold leaves all interpretive responsibility to the
teacher. Detection of heavily post-edited or deeply paraphrased texts remains a
partial blind spot, even if Magister+ now addresses semantic paraphrases.
Inter-analysis variability for the same document is officially acknowledged.

**Key difference Studium vs Magister+**: Studium provides an AI score without the
advanced features (semantic detection, multilingual, altered texts, grammar report).
It is a student self-check tool, not a sanctioning tool. Magister+ is the complete
institutional tool — but it remains a **decision-support tool**, never proof on
its own.

## Calibration Implications for Natural Writing

| Compilatio tier encountered | Relevant formulas | Priority action |
|---|---|---|
| **Studium** (student self-check) | Formulas 1–27 (Tier 1 surface) | Burstiness (B), AI markers, TTR variance |
| **Magister** (teacher, no AI detection) | Similarity only — not AI detection | Focus on source originality |
| **Magister+** (teacher, full pipeline) | Formulas 1–38 + semantic layer | All surface metrics + deep paraphrase + student anchoring (Step 0) |

The semantic detection layer in Magister+ is the most consequential addition: it
targets paraphrases that defeat syntactic detection. The only reliable counter is
genuine content from the student's lived experience — data that no LLM can possess.
