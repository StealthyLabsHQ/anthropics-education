## 1. Essay: complete method

### Phase 1: Analyze the prompt (15-20 min)

**Step 1: Read three times.**
1st reading: grasp the overall meaning.
2nd reading: underline keywords.
3rd reading: identify the instruction (discuss, analyze, show that, to what extent...).

**Step 2: Define each keyword.**
No definition = no grade. A word can have several meanings; choose the one relevant to the context of the prompt.

Example, prompt: "Does technological progress destroy jobs?"
- Technological progress: the set of innovations (products, processes, organization) that increase productivity
- Destroys: permanently eliminates (≠ transforms, displaces)
- Jobs: paid work, broadly defined (not just permanent contracts)

**Step 3: Restate the prompt as an explicit question.**
"Does technological progress destroy jobs?" → "Do technological innovations lead to a net loss of jobs, or do they instead bring about a restructuring of the labor market?"

**Step 4: Delimit.**
What is IN scope:
- The link between technology and employment
- Quantitative effects (number of jobs) and qualitative effects (nature of jobs)

What is OUT OF scope:
- Technological progress in general (without connection to employment)
- Employment in general (without connection to technology)

### Phase 2: Build the outline (15-20 min)

**Brainstorm.** On a rough draft, list everything that comes to mind:
authors, theories, examples, statistics. Then organize.

**Choose the type of outline:**

#### Dialectical outline
For prompts such as "Is X Y?", "Can we say that...?", "Discuss"

```
I. Thesis: Yes, technological progress destroys jobs
   A. Automation of repetitive tasks (example: self-checkout machines)
   B. Statistical data (Frey & Osborne: 47% of US jobs at risk)
   C. Disappearance of entire professions (historical example: weavers)

II. Antithesis: No, it creates new ones
   A. Compensation theory (Sauvy): destroyed jobs are replaced
   B. New sectors (example: developers, data scientists)
   C. Productivity effect → lower prices → increased demand → jobs

III. Synthesis / Transcendence: It transforms them
   A. The real question is that of transition (skills, training)
   B. Inequalities in the face of technological change (labor market polarization)
   C. The role of public policy in supporting the transition
```

The synthesis is NOT a summary of I + II. It is a transcendence that shows
that the initial question was poorly framed or incomplete.

#### Analytical outline
For prompts such as "Analyze", "Explain", "What are the causes of..."

```
I. Observation: description of the phenomenon
II. Causes / Explanations
III. Consequences / Implications
```

#### Thematic outline
For broad prompts with no obvious opposition

```
I. Axis 1 (economic dimension)
II. Axis 2 (social dimension)
III. Axis 3 (political dimension)
```

### Phase 3: Write the introduction (10-15 min)

Mandatory order:

1. **Hook:** A fact, a statistic, a quotation, a recent event.
   NOT a platitude ("Since the dawn of time, humanity...").

2. **Presentation of the topic:** Contextualize, show its importance.

3. **Definition of key terms:** Clarify what is being discussed.

4. **Research question:** The question the prompt actually raises.

5. **Outline announcement:** WITHOUT writing "First... second... finally..."

Example:

> In 2023, an IMF study estimated that 40% of global jobs would be affected
> by artificial intelligence. This figure reignites an old debate: the relationship
> between technological progress (that is, the set of innovations that increase
> productivity) and employment. Does technology eliminate positions irreversibly,
> or does it mainly trigger a restructuring of the labor market? While automation
> does threaten certain job categories, the dynamics of creation and transformation
> it generates paint a more nuanced picture, where the decisive variable is less
> technology itself than the capacity of societies to manage the transition.

### Phase 4: Write the body

**Golden rule: 1 paragraph = 1 argument.**

Structure of each paragraph:
1. Topic sentence (1 sentence)
2. Explanation / development (2-3 sentences)
3. Specific example (fact, statistic, author) (1-2 sentences)
4. Mini-conclusion / link to the section (1 sentence)

**Transitions between sections:** Mini-summary of the ending section + announcement of what follows.
No mechanical transition. Show the logical link.

### Phase 5: Write the conclusion (5-10 min)

1. **Synthesis:** Summarize the argument by showing its progression (don't just repeat the outline)
2. **Answer to the research question:** Clear, direct, nuanced
3. **Opening:** A genuine extension (not "And what about globalization then?")

## 2. Text commentary: complete method

### Phase 1: Read and annotate (15 min)

1. **1st reading:** grasp the overall meaning. What is the text about?
2. **2nd reading:** identify the theme, the author's thesis, the tone, the genre
3. **3rd reading:** spot the techniques (style, figures, structure, lexical fields)
4. **Annotate:** in the margin, note the effects produced by each technique

### Phase 2: Identify the research question

The research question in a commentary concerns the HOW, not the WHAT:
- BAD: "What is this text about?"
- GOOD: "How does the author achieve [effect] through [techniques]?"

### Phase 3: Build the outline

**Axis-based outline** (most common, recommended):
2-3 thematic axes corresponding to the main effects/issues of the text.
Each axis combines content (what the text says) and form (how it says it).

**Linear outline** (sometimes required):
Follow the order of the text, movement by movement.
Risk: paraphrase. Each movement must be analyzed, not summarized.

### Phase 4: Write

**Commentary introduction:**
1. Presentation of the work and author (1-2 sentences, not a biography)
2. Location of the passage within the work (if an extract)
3. Summary of the passage in 1-2 sentences
4. Research question
5. Outline announcement

**Body: the PCI reflex:**
For each argument:
- **P**rocess: name the technique (metaphor, anaphora, lexical field, rhythm...)
- **C**itation: quote the precise passage in quotation marks
- **I**nterpretation: what effect it produces, why the author makes that choice

NEVER name a technique without a quotation. NEVER quote without interpretation.

## 3. Case commentary (law)

### Structure

```
Introduction
├── Hook (legal context)
├── Facts (summarized, chronological)
├── Procedure (trial court, court of appeal, appeal)
├── Legal issue (the legal question decided)
├── Court's ruling (decision + reasoning)
├── Outline announcement

I. [First aspect of the ruling]
   A. [Principle established / rule applied]
   B. [Scope / implications]

II. [Second aspect of the ruling]
   A. [Court's reasoning]
   B. [Critical evaluation]
```

### Specific pitfalls
- Do NOT narrate the facts like a story (stay legal)
- Do NOT paraphrase the ruling (analyze its scope)
- Cite statutory articles precisely
- Compare with prior case law if possible
- Give your critical opinion (in master's programs, this is expected)

## 4. Document study (history-geography)

### Structure

```
Introduction
├── Nature of the document (text, map, photo, graph, poster...)
├── Author, date, historical context
├── Main subject / theme
├── Research question (what does this document tell us about...?)
├── Outline announcement

Body (2-3 thematic sections)
├── Each section: idea + quotation/description from document + analysis + contextualization

Conclusion
├── Interest of the document
├── Limits (what it doesn't show, author's bias)
├── Historical significance
```

### Error #1: paraphrase
Do not narrate the document. Analyze it. "The document shows X" is not analysis.
"The document shows X, which is explained by Y in the context of Z" is.

## 5. Hook and transition bank

### Hooks: types that work

| Type | Example | When to use |
|------|---------|-------------|
| Striking statistic | "In 2024, 60% of French people..." | When recent data exists |
| Quotation | "As Tocqueville wrote..." | If the quote is short and relevant |
| Current event | "The passing of law X in March 2024..." | If current events are directly related |
| Paradox | "France produces more than ever, but..." | For a prompt with internal tension |
| Question | "Can we still speak of... when...?" | If the question is incisive |

### Hooks to ABSOLUTELY AVOID

- "Since the dawn of time, humanity has..."
- "Since time immemorial..."
- "The dictionary defines X as..."
- "X is a subject that generates a lot of discussion"
- Any hook that could introduce any prompt whatsoever

### Transitions: natural formulations

Instead of "Having examined X, we will now look at Y":

- "X alone is not enough to explain the phenomenon. The question of Y remains."
- "If X is established, it must be qualified: Y complicates the picture."
- "This analysis has its limits. Y forces us to reconsider."
- [End the section with a question, open the next with a fact that answers it]

## 6. Errors penalized by markers

| Error | Impact on grade | How to avoid |
|--------|-------------------|----------------|
| Off-topic | -4 to -8 points | Reread the prompt every 30 min while writing |
| No research question | -3 to -5 points | Formulate the question before writing anything |
| List-based outline (no logic) | -3 to -4 points | Check that each section answers part of the research question |
| No examples | -2 to -3 points | Minimum 1 example per sub-section |
| Paraphrase (commentary) | -3 to -5 points | Always analyze the "why" and "how", not the "what" |
| Conclusion repeating the introduction | -1 to -2 points | The conclusion provides an answer; the introduction poses the question |
| Repeated language errors | -1 to -3 points | Proofread twice: once for content, once for language |
| Detected copy-paste | 0/20 or disciplinary hearing | Never copy. Rewrite, quote, cite sources. |
