## 1. Effective revision methods

### Active recall

The brain retains information better when it PRODUCES it rather than simply RE-READS it.

**In practice:**
- Read the course once, then close the material and try to recall from memory
- Ask yourself questions: "What are the 3 principles of X?" BEFORE checking
- Use flashcards (Anki, paper, or simply a notebook folded in half)
- Explain the concept to someone (or out loud, alone)

**What does NOT work (or works poorly):**
- Reading the course 5 times while highlighting → illusion of mastery
- Copying the course word for word → mechanical work, not cognitive
- Looking at answers without having attempted the exercise first

### Spaced repetition

More effective to revise 4 times for 20 minutes over 4 days than once for 80 minutes.

Typical schedule for a concept:
- Day 1: learn
- Day 2: revise (10 min)
- Day 4: revise (5 min)
- Day 7: revise (5 min)
- Day 14: final test

Free tool: Anki (flashcards with built-in spaced repetition algorithm).

### Interleaving

Do not revise a single subject for 3 hours. Alternate:
- 45 min of accounting → 45 min of law → 45 min of accounting
- Mix exercise types within the same session

The brain learns better when it must distinguish and categorize.

### The Feynman technique

1. Write the concept at the top of a sheet
2. Explain it in simple terms, as if to a 12-year-old
3. Identify where the explanation breaks down
4. Return to the course for those exact points
5. Simplify further

If you cannot explain it simply, you have not understood it.

## 2. Planning your revision

### Revision planning: method

**Step 1: Inventory.** List all subjects and chapters to revise.
Estimate the time needed per chapter (light / medium / heavy).

**Step 2: Prioritize.**
| Priority | Criterion |
|----------|---------|
| High | High coefficient + weak mastery |
| Medium | High coefficient + good mastery OR low coefficient + weak mastery |
| Low | Low coefficient + good mastery |

**Step 3: Spread over time.**

Example: 2 weeks before exams, 5 subjects

```
Week 1 (foundations):
  Mon: Subject A (heavy chapter) + Subject B (light chapter)
  Tue: Subject C (heavy chapter)
  Wed: Subject A (medium chapter) + Subject D
  Thu: Subject B (heavy chapter) + Subject E
  Fri: Subject C (medium chapter) + Subject A exercises
  Sat: B + C exercises
  Sun: Rest or light revision of weak points

Week 2 (consolidation):
  Mon: Past papers Subject A
  Tue: Past papers Subject B + C
  Wed: Identified weak points + sheets
  Thu: Past papers Subject D + E
  Fri: Final quick revision (sheets only)
  Sat: Timed mock exam
  Sun: Rest
```

### The Pomodoro planning method for revision

- 25 min of focused work
- 5 min break (get up, drink, look out the window, NOT the phone)
- After 4 cycles: 15-20 min break
- Goal: 8-12 Pomodoros per day of intense revision

## 3. Revision sheets: formats by subject

### Conceptual subjects (philosophy, social sciences, sociology, management...)

```
SHEET: [Subject] | [Theme / Chapter]

KEY AUTHORS
• [Author 1] ([date]): [theory in 1 sentence], Work: [title]
• [Author 2] ([date]): [theory in 1 sentence], Work: [title]

CONCEPTS
• [Concept 1]: [short definition]
  → Related to: [other concept]
  → Example: [concrete illustration]

DEBATES / OPPOSITIONS
• [Author A] vs [Author B] on [question]:
  A says... / B says... / The nuance is...

TYPICAL OUTLINE (if the theme comes up as an essay)
I. [Axis 1]
II. [Axis 2]
III. [Axis 3]

USEFUL QUOTATIONS (max 3)
• "[quotation]", [Author], [Work], [date]
```

### Technical subjects (accounting, tax law, finance, mathematics...)

```
SHEET: [Subject] | [Theme]

FORMULAS / RULES
• [Formula 1]: [name] = [expression]
  → When to use: [context]
  → Pitfall: [common error]

• [Legal / accounting rule]: Article [X] of [Code]
  → Principle: [in 1 sentence]
  → Exception: [if any]

TYPICAL METHOD
1. Identify [what]
2. Calculate / Apply [formula/rule]
3. Verify [how]
4. Conclude [with what wording]

WORKED EXAMPLE
Problem: [short case]
Step 1: [calculation / reasoning]
Step 2: [next step]
Result: [answer]

COMMON ERRORS
• Confusing [X] and [Y] → the difference is: ...
• Forgetting [critical step]
• Applying [rule] when [exception applies]
```

### Memory-based subjects (history, geography, biology...)

```
SHEET: [Subject] | [Theme]

CHRONOLOGY / KEY DATES
• [Date 1]: [event] → significance: [why it matters]
• [Date 2]: [event] → link to: [previous event]

KEY ACTORS
• [Person / country / institution]: role in [context]

CAUSES → EVENT → CONSEQUENCES
[Causal diagram in 3 steps]

SPECIFIC VOCABULARY
• [Term 1]: [definition]
• [Term 2]: [definition]

MIND MAP (textual description)
[Central concept]
├── Branch 1: [sub-theme]
│   ├── [detail]
│   └── [detail]
├── Branch 2: [sub-theme]
└── Branch 3: [sub-theme]
```

## 4. Preparation by exam type

### Multiple choice questions (MCQs)

- Read ALL the options before answering
- Watch for double negatives ("which is NOT incorrect")
- When in doubt, eliminate obviously wrong answers first
- Check whether there are negative marks (if so, do not guess randomly)
- Revise DETAILS: MCQs test precision, not global understanding

### Practical case (law, accounting, management)

Response structure:
1. **Legally / accountably classify** the facts
2. **Identify the applicable rule** (article, standard, principle)
3. **Apply** the rule to the facts
4. **Conclude** (answer to the question asked)

Pitfall: do not launch into calculations or reasoning before first
classifying the situation. Classification is 50% of the grade.

### Essay (in exam conditions)

- Read the prompt 3 times
- Draft: 30-40% of total time
- Writing: 50-60%
- Proofreading: 10%
- If time is short: write the introduction + conclusion in full,
  write the topic sentences of each section without developing them

### Oral / Presentation

- Prepare structured notes (outline + keywords), not a written-out text
- Practice in front of a mirror or by recording yourself
- Speak slowly (you always speak faster than you think)
- Prepare 3 questions the panel might ask
- Have a memorized closing sentence to finish cleanly

## 5. Stress management and time management on exam day

### Before the exam

- Sleep (the brain consolidates memory during sleep)
- Eat a proper meal (no fasting, no overeating)
- Arrive early to settle in calmly
- Review your sheets the evening before, not on the morning of the exam (for anxious students)

### During the exam

**The first 5 minutes:**
1. Read the ENTIRE paper / all instructions
2. Take 3 deep breaths
3. Identify what you know how to do and what will be difficult
4. Start with what you master (confidence + secured points)

**Time management:**
- Write the end times for each exercise/section at the top of your paper
- If blocked: move on and come back later
- Keep 10 min for proofreading: NON-NEGOTIABLE

**If you panic:**
- Put the pen down
- Breathe: inhale 4 sec, hold 4 sec, exhale 4 sec
- Remember: the panel is not looking for perfection, they are looking for method and reasoning
- Writing something is always better than leaving a blank
