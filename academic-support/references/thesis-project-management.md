# Thesis Project Management

**When to load this reference**: the student asks for help organizing their thesis schedule, planning meetings with their supervisor/advisor, overcoming a writing block (blank page, procrastination, impostor syndrome), or setting up a document versioning system.

---

## 1. Typical timeline by level

### BUT / Vocational Bachelor's (2-3 months)

| Week | Milestone | Deliverable |
|---------|-------|----------|
| W1-W2 | Topic selection and validation | Signed topic form |
| W3-W4 | Documentary research | Annotated bibliography (10-15 sources) |
| W5-W6 | Detailed outline writing | Detailed outline validated by supervisor |
| W7-W8 | Body writing | Complete draft (introduction, sections, conclusion) |
| W9-W10 | Proofreading and corrections | Proofread version, formatted |
| W11-W12 | Finalization and submission | Final document + thesis defense preparation |

### Master's 1 (4 months)

| Week | Milestone | Deliverable |
|---------|-------|----------|
| W1-W2 | Topic framing | Framing sheet (topic, research question, intended method) |
| W3-W4 | Initial literature review | Reading note for each key source (15-20 sources) |
| W5-W6 | Theoretical framework construction | Detailed outline + provisional introduction |
| W7-W8 | Data collection / fieldwork | Interview guide, questionnaire, or protocol |
| W9-W10 | Results analysis | Results chapter (draft) |
| W11-W12 | Discussion section writing | Discussion + limitations (draft) |
| W13-W14 | Full proofreading | Complete proofread version |
| W15-W16 | Final corrections and submission | Final document + thesis defense preparation |

**Recommended intermediate deliverable**: submit chapters 1 and 2 to the supervisor at the end of W8 to get feedback before continuing.

### Master's 2 Research (6 months)

| Week | Milestone | Deliverable |
|---------|-------|----------|
| W1-W3 | Framing and preliminary literature review | Framing note + initial bibliography (30+ sources) |
| W4-W6 | In-depth literature review | Literature review chapter (draft) |
| W7-W9 | Theoretical framework and methodology | Methodology chapter (draft) |
| W10-W12 | Data collection / experimentation | Raw data, field journal |
| W13-W15 | Results analysis | Results chapter (draft) |
| W16-W18 | Discussion and conclusion writing | Discussion + conclusion chapters (draft) |
| W19-W21 | Full proofreading, general introduction | Complete integrated version |
| W22-W24 | Corrections, formatting, submission | Final document + thesis defense preparation |

**Intermediate deliverable 1**: literature review chapter at W6.
**Intermediate deliverable 2**: methodology + results chapters at W15.

## 2. Meetings with the supervisor

### Recommended frequency

| Level | Frequency | Note |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| BUT / Vocational Bachelor's | Every 2-3 weeks | The supervisor often initiates meetings |
| Master's 1 | Every 2-3 weeks | Take the initiative to request meetings |
| Master's 2 Research | Every 2 weeks minimum | More frequent during field and final writing phases |

### What to prepare before each meeting

Arriving empty-handed to a meeting is wasting everyone's time. Before each meeting, prepare:

1. **A progress update**: what has been done since the last meeting (even if little).
2. **Precise questions**: not "I'm stuck", but "I'm unsure whether to use approach A or B to analyze my data; here are the arguments for each".
3. **Identified blockers**: describe the problem concretely and what has already been tried.
4. **An up-to-date document**: send the latest version of the thesis 48 hours before the meeting so the supervisor has time to read it.

### Email template for requesting a meeting

```
Subject: Thesis [SHORT TITLE] - Meeting request

Dear [Mr./Ms. NAME],

I am writing to arrange a meeting regarding
my thesis on [topic in one line].

Since our last exchange, I have made progress on the following points:
- [point 1]
- [point 2]

I would appreciate your input on:
- [question 1]
- [question 2]

Would you be available in the next two weeks? I am
free [suggest 2-3 time slots]. I will send you my latest version
of the document before our meeting.

Thank you in advance,
[First name LAST NAME]
[Program, year]
```

### Meeting minutes template

Send this summary to the supervisor within 24 hours of the meeting.

```
MEETING MINUTES - THESIS
==================================
Date: [DD/MM/YYYY]
Present: [student], [supervisor]
Duration: [XX min]

POINTS DISCUSSED
---------------
1. [Topic discussed]
   - [What was said / decided]

2. [Topic discussed]
   - [What was said / decided]

DECISIONS MADE
----------------
- [Decision 1]
- [Decision 2]

NEXT STEPS (for the student)
------------------------------------
- [ ] [Task 1] - deadline: [date]
- [ ] [Task 2] - deadline: [date]
- [ ] [Task 3] - deadline: [date]

NEXT MEETING
-----------------
Scheduled date: [DD/MM/YYYY] or to be arranged before [date]
```

### How to manage an unavailable supervisor

This is a common situation. Here are concrete strategies:

- **Be proactive**: don't wait for the supervisor to propose a meeting. Send an email with specific time slots.
- **Send brief progress updates by email**: even without a meeting, a 5-line email every two weeks shows that work is progressing and maintains the relationship.
- **Ask closed questions**: instead of "what do you think?", write "I'm torn between A and B; I lean toward A because [reason]. Does that seem right to you?" The supervisor can reply in one line.
- **Set clear deadlines**: "I plan to finish chapter 2 by March 15; could you give me feedback by March 22?"
- **Have a backup plan**: identify a co-supervisor, a PhD student in the lab, or an advanced peer who can proofread in the meantime.
- **As a last resort**: if the lack of feedback is jeopardizing the timeline, alert the program coordinator or student services. It is a right, not a betrayal.

## 3. Managing blocks

### Blank page

The blank page is not a competence problem: it is a starting problem. Here are techniques that work:

- **Write badly first**: give yourself permission to write a terrible draft. The goal is to fill the page, not produce a perfect text. Corrections come later. A bad paragraph is always better than a non-existent paragraph.
- **Pomodoro technique**: set a timer for 25 minutes. During those 25 minutes, write without stopping, without rereading, without correcting. 5-minute break. Repeat. After 4 cycles, a long break of 15-20 minutes.
- **Start with the easiest section**: nothing requires writing in order. If the methodology is clear, start there. The introduction is often written last.
- **Talk before writing**: explain out loud (or to a friend) what you want to say, then write what you just said. Spoken language is more fluid than written language.
- **List before writing**: write 5-10 points to address in the section as a list, then turn each point into a paragraph.

### Procrastination

Procrastination often comes from tasks that are too large and too vague. The solution: break them down.

- **25-minute micro-tasks**: instead of "write chapter 2", write "write the paragraph on the definition of concept X (25 min)".
- **2-minute rule**: if a task takes less than 2 minutes (sending an email, adding a reference), do it immediately.
- **Daily to-do list**: maximum 3 thesis-related tasks per day. No more, to avoid the discouraging effect of an endless list.
- **Ritualize the work**: same place, same time, same start-up routine (open the file, reread the last paragraph, write).

### Perfectionism

Perfectionism is the enemy of the finished thesis. Remember:

> **A submitted thesis is worth more than a perfect thesis never finished.**

- The first version does not need to be good. It needs to exist.
- The supervisor is there to correct and guide. Sending imperfect work lets them do their job.
- Corrections are part of the normal process. Nobody writes a thesis in a single pass.
- Set a limit: maximum 2 rounds of self-review per chapter before sending to the supervisor. Beyond that, you are going in circles.

### Impostor syndrome

Impostor syndrome is **normal and frequent** among students writing a thesis. Typical thoughts:

- "I don't understand my topic well enough to write about it."
- "Everything has already been said; I bring nothing new."
- "My supervisor will see that I'm not up to the task."

Responses to tell yourself:

- **You don't need to understand everything before writing. You write to understand.** Writing is a thinking tool, not just a finished product.
- A master's thesis is not a PhD. A revolutionary contribution is not expected: a rigorous approach is.
- The supervisor knows you are a student. They do not expect perfection; they expect work and progression.
- Talking about your doubts (supervisor, peers, loved ones) is healthy. Keeping them to yourself amplifies them.

### When to alert the supervisor or student services

Some situations require action:

- **A block of more than 2-3 weeks** with no written output, despite the techniques above.
- **Access problems with the field** (refusal of interviews, unavailable data) that call the feasibility of the thesis into question.
- **Personal problems** (health, financial, family) that prevent work. Universities have support services: social workers, health services, academic accommodations.
- **Conflict with the supervisor**: talk to the program coordinator, not on social media.
- **Significant delay on the schedule** (more than 3 weeks behind the initial plan): better to flag it early than to discover the problem at submission time.

## 4. Document versioning

### Naming convention

Use a clear and consistent naming scheme:

```
Thesis_LASTNAME_v1.2_2024-03-15.docx
```

Where:
- `LASTNAME`: student's last name
- `v1.2`: version number (major.minor)
  - **Major version** (v1, v2, v3): significant restructuring, complete new chapter
  - **Minor version** (v1.1, v1.2): corrections, added paragraphs, local changes
- `2024-03-15`: date in ISO format (year-month-day) for easy chronological sorting

Concrete examples:

```
Thesis_SMITH_v0.1_2024-01-20.docx    <- first detailed outline
Thesis_SMITH_v0.5_2024-02-10.docx    <- partial draft
Thesis_SMITH_v1.0_2024-03-01.docx    <- first complete version
Thesis_SMITH_v1.1_2024-03-10.docx    <- after supervisor feedback
Thesis_SMITH_v2.0_2024-03-25.docx    <- major restructuring
Thesis_SMITH_vFINAL_2024-04-15.docx  <- submitted version
```

### Golden rule: never overwrite a file

**Never save over a previous version.** Always use "Save As" with a new version number. If something breaks, you can always go back.

Create an `_archives` folder to store old versions and keep the main folder clean:

```
Thesis/
  Thesis_SMITH_v2.1_2024-03-20.docx    <- current working version
  _archives/
    Thesis_SMITH_v0.1_2024-01-20.docx
    Thesis_SMITH_v1.0_2024-03-01.docx
    Thesis_SMITH_v1.1_2024-03-10.docx
    Thesis_SMITH_v2.0_2024-03-15.docx
```

### Cloud backup

Use **OneDrive**, **Google Drive**, or **Nextcloud** (often provided by the university):

- Enable **automatic synchronization** of the thesis folder.
- Check that **version history** is enabled (Google Drive and OneDrive do this by default). This allows restoring a previous version even if you accidentally saved over it.
- Do not rely solely on the cloud: it is not a backup, it is a sync. If you delete a file locally, it disappears from the cloud too.

### Simplified 3-2-1 backup rule

The professional 3-2-1 rule adapted for students:

- **3 copies** of the file: one on the computer, one in the cloud, one on external storage.
- **2 different media**: the computer's hard drive + a USB drive or external hard drive.
- **1 remote copy**: the cloud (Google Drive, OneDrive, etc.).

In practice, the vital minimum:

```
1. Computer           -> daily work
2. Google Drive/OneDrive -> automatic sync
3. USB drive          -> manual copy 1x per week (or at each major version)
```

Set a weekly reminder in your calendar: "Back up thesis to USB drive."

### Git for computer science students

For computer science students or those writing in LaTeX, **Git** is a far more powerful versioning tool:

- Every change is tracked with a commit message.
- You can return to any previous state of the document.
- You can work on branches (for example a branch to test a restructuring).
- Remote repository on **GitHub** or **GitLab** (universities often provide a GitLab instance).

This goes beyond the scope of this sheet, but it is a useful skill to develop for technical profiles.

## 5. Integrating supervisor feedback

Receiving comments on your work can be unsettling. Here is a method for handling them effectively.

### Step 1: Read all comments before making changes

Do not start correcting at the first comment you read. Read through all the feedback to get a global picture. Some comments overlap; others contradict each other (the supervisor may have changed their mind after reading further).

### Step 2: Classify the comments

Create three categories:

| Type | Description | Example | Priority |
|------|-------------|---------|----------|
| **Structural** | Concerns organization, the outline, overall logic | "This section should come before the methodology" | High |
| **Stylistic** | Concerns writing style, clarity, grammar | "Rewrite this sentence, too long" | Medium |
| **Suggestion** | Supplementary idea, reference to add, avenue to explore | "You could also cite X (2020)" | Low |

### Step 3: Handle structural issues first

Structural changes (moving sections, merging chapters, deleting a section) affect everything else. Doing them first avoids perfecting a paragraph that will be deleted later.

Recommended order:

1. Structural changes (outline, organization)
2. Content changes (arguments, data, references)
3. Stylistic changes (style, clarity)
4. Formatting corrections (spelling, layout)

### Step 4: Don't rewrite everything at each round of feedback

Supervisor feedback does not mean starting over. Identify what needs to change and what can stay as is. If the supervisor does not comment on a section, it is generally acceptable.

Pitfall to avoid: completely rewriting a chapter after every round of feedback, risking introducing new problems and never converging on a final version.

### Step 5: Track changes

- **In Word**: use **Track Changes** (Review tab > Track Changes). The supervisor will see exactly what changed.
- **In Google Docs**: use **Suggestion mode** for changes and **comments** for replies.
- **In LaTeX**: use the `changes` or `latexdiff` package to generate a document showing differences.

### Step 6: Reply to the supervisor

After integrating the feedback, send a summary email or document. The supervisor will appreciate knowing what was taken on board.

```
Subject: Thesis [TOPIC] - Response to your comments (v1.1 -> v1.2)

Dear [Mr./Ms. NAME],

Please find attached the updated version of my thesis (v1.2)
incorporating your feedback from [date].

Here is a summary of the changes:

CHANGES MADE
- [Comment 1]: addressed. I moved section X before section Y as suggested.
- [Comment 2]: addressed. I added the reference to X (2020)
  and developed the argument on p.12.
- [Comment 3]: addressed. Sentence rewritten on p.8.

POINTS NOT CHANGED (with justification)
- [Comment 4]: I chose to keep the current structure
  because [reason]. I am open to discussing this if you feel
  it is necessary.

OUTSTANDING QUESTIONS
- [Question 1]
- [Question 2]

Please do not hesitate to share any further remarks.

Best regards,
[First name LAST NAME]
```

This summary shows the supervisor that their comments were read, considered, and addressed seriously. It also speeds up exchanges because the supervisor does not need to reread everything to check whether their feedback was taken into account.
