# The Synthesis Paper

> **When to load this reference**: the student is working on a synthesis paper, a document synthesis exercise, a multi-document summary, or preparing a competitive exam (civil service, university admissions) that includes this type of exercise.

---

## 1. Differences between similar exercises

| Exercise | Objective | Personal position | Sources |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Synthesis paper** | Synthesize a documentary file | **None** (absolute neutrality) | **Only** the documents provided |
| **Summary** | Condense **a single** text following a ratio (e.g. 1/4) | None, fidelity to the text | The single text to summarize |
| **Essay** | Argue a personal thesis | **Yes** (taking a position is expected) | Personal knowledge + external sources |
| **Commentary** | Analyze a specific text or ruling | Critical analysis, not free opinion | The text/ruling being commented |

**Cardinal rule of the synthesis paper**: never give your opinion, never add knowledge external to the file. Markers penalize any deviation immediately.

---

## 2. Document analysis method

Document analysis is the most important step. Rushed analysis inevitably leads to a "catalogue" outline (document by document), which is the leading cause of poor grades.

### 2.1 Quick reading (30-40 min for a 20-25 page file)

1. **Read the cover page**: topic, instructions, number of documents, expected length.
2. **Skim each document**: title, header, source, date, introduction, conclusion.
3. **Identify the nature of each document**: legislative text, press article, report, book excerpt, statistical data, case law, op-ed, etc.
4. **Identify the general theme** of the file and begin mentally formulating a research question.

### 2.2 In-depth reading and analysis table (45-60 min)

For each document, fill in the analysis table (see template in section 7). You must:

- Identify the main theme and key ideas (2-4 per document).
- Identify the author's position (for, against, nuanced, descriptive).
- Highlight or annotate useful passages with the document number.
- **Systematically number** each document (Doc. 1, Doc. 2, etc.) if not already done.

### 2.3 Cross-referencing documents (20-30 min)

- Identify **convergences**: which documents defend similar ideas?
- Identify **divergences**: which documents oppose or qualify each other?
- Identify **cross-cutting themes** that run across several documents.
- Group key ideas by theme, not by document.

## 3. Building the synthesis outline

### 3.1 Core principles

- The synthesis paper outline is **NOT** an essay outline.
- Never use a thesis / antithesis / synthesis structure.
- Never use a "catalogue" outline (Doc. 1, then Doc. 2, then Doc. 3...).
- The outline is **thematic**: each section corresponds to a major cross-cutting idea.
- **Each section must draw on several documents**: this is the key criterion for success.

### 3.2 Recommended structure

- **2 to 3 sections** maximum.
- **2 to 3 sub-sections** per section.
- Balance sections by volume.

### 3.3 Common outline types

| Outline type | When to use | Example structure |
|---|---|---|
| **Thematic** | Most common case (the file addresses several aspects of a topic) | I. Aspect A / II. Aspect B / III. Aspect C |
| **Chronological** | The file highlights a development over time | I. Initial situation / II. Evolution / III. Current situation |
| **Problem / Solutions** | The file identifies a problem and proposes responses | I. Statement of the problem / II. Proposed solutions (and their limits) |
| **Causes / Consequences** | The file analyzes a phenomenon and its effects | I. Origins of the phenomenon / II. Observed consequences |

### 3.4 Formulating the research question

The research question is not a simple question. It must:

- Emerge from the cross-referencing of documents (not be imposed from outside).
- Pose a tension, an issue that justifies the existence of the file.
- Make it possible to logically organize the outline.

---

## 4. Writing

### 4.1 Introduction (15-20 lines)

The introduction has three mandatory components:

1. **Hook and context**: present the file's theme and its current relevance. Draw on the documents without citing them one by one.
2. **Research question**: clearly formulate the central question that emerges from the file.
3. **Outline announcement**: present the main sections fluidly (avoid "in a first section... in a second section...").

**Do not**: list the documents, give your opinion, use a hook outside the file.

### 4.2 Body

Each paragraph follows this structure:

1. **Topic sentence**: opening sentence that announces the paragraph's idea.
2. **Document contributions**: what the documents say about this idea, with references. Draw on at least 2-3 documents per sub-section.
3. **Articulation**: transition to the next idea.

#### References to documents

- Cite documents in parentheses: **(doc. 1)**, **(doc. 3 and 5)**, **(doc. 2, 4 and 7)**.
- Vary formulations: "According to the author of document 3...", "The report presented in document 5 notes that...", "As document 1 indicates...".
- **Systematically paraphrase**: never copy full sentences. Copying from the file is penalized.

### 4.3 Conclusion (10-15 lines)

- **Synthetic summary**: recall the main lines of the response provided to the research question.
- **No personal opening**: unlike an essay, do not close with a question or personal reflection.
- You may optionally highlight an unresolved tension between documents, but without taking sides.

### 4.4 Length and formatting

- **Usual length**: 4 to 6 pages (follow the instruction: it is mandatory).
- Skip a line between sections, not between sub-sections.
- Paragraph indent at the start.
- No visible headings in the copy (unless instructed otherwise).
- Neutral and impersonal writing: no "I", no opinion "we".

---

## 5. Time management under exam conditions

For a **4-hour** exam (the most common duration):

| Step | Recommended time | Cumulative |
|---|---|---|
| Reading cover page and skimming file | 10 min | 0h10 |
| In-depth reading and analysis table | 50 min | 1h00 |
| Cross-referencing documents and identifying themes | 20 min | 1h20 |
| Building the detailed outline and research question | 25 min | 1h45 |
| Writing the introduction | 15 min | 2h00 |
| Writing the body | 1h30 | 3h30 |
| Writing the conclusion | 10 min | 3h40 |
| Proofreading and corrections | 20 min | 4h00 |

For a **3-hour** exam, reduce proportionally, mainly by compressing the writing phase (be more concise).

**Advice**: never start writing before having a complete detailed outline. Time invested in analysis and the outline always pays off.

---

## 6. Pitfalls to avoid

| Pitfall | Why it is penalized | How to avoid it |
|---|---|---|
| **Taking a personal position** | The synthesis paper requires absolute neutrality | Ban "I think", "one should", "we ought to": use impersonal constructions |
| **Catalogue outline (document by document)** | Shows an inability to cross-reference sources | Build the outline around themes, not documents |
| **Adding external knowledge** | The file is the only allowed material | If an idea is not in the documents, do not include it |
| **Copying sentences from documents** | Plagiarism, paraphrasing is mandatory | Close the document, rewrite in your own words, then verify accuracy |
| **Forgetting a document from the file** | Every document must be used at least once | Check off each document as it is used |
| **Exceeding the required length** | Lack of synthesis ability, a disqualifying flaw | Count pages/words regularly while writing |
| **Unbalanced treatment of documents** | One document should not occupy half the copy | Verify that each section draws on several documents in a balanced way |
| **No research question** | The synthesis becomes a scattered summary | Always formulate a central question that organizes the outline |

## 8. Annotated sample outline

**Fictional file**: 5 documents on the digital transformation of French SMEs.

- **Doc. 1:** INSEE Report (2023): statistical overview of SME digitization.
- **Doc. 2:** Article (2024): testimonies from SME managers on difficulties encountered.
- **Doc. 3:** Parliamentary report: public measures to support digitization.
- **Doc. 4:** Op-ed by an economics researcher: risks of the digital divide between large firms and SMEs.
- **Doc. 5:** Case study (specialist journal): success of an SME that integrated AI into its processes.

### Research question derived

*To what extent does the digital transformation of SMEs constitute both a competitiveness challenge and a hurdle to overcome?*

### Detailed outline

**I. Digital transformation of SMEs: a contrasted picture**

- **I.1 A proven but variable digital lag depending on the sector**
  - INSEE data (doc. 1): gap between basic adoption (website) and advanced tools (cloud, AI).
  - The researcher (doc. 4) confirms the risk of falling behind large firms and international competition.

- **I.2 Multiple obstacles slowing the transition**
  - Manager testimonies (doc. 2): cost, lack of skills, resistance to change.
  - The parliamentary report (doc. 3) acknowledges these barriers.
  - The researcher (doc. 4) highlights the insufficiency of current programs.

**II. Levers and prospects for a successful digital transition**

- **II.1 Public policies and support programs**
  - Measures listed by the parliament (doc. 3): financial support, training, one-stop shop.
  - The case study (doc. 5) shows the importance of external support.
  - But the researcher (doc. 4) considers these measures insufficient given the scale of the challenge.

- **II.2 Success conditions at company level**
  - The case study (doc. 5): gradual investment, measurable productivity gains.
  - Need to train teams (doc. 2 and 3).
  - Pragmatic approach rather than a wholesale overhaul (convergence of doc. 2, 3 and 5).

### Comments on this outline

- **Each sub-section draws on at least 2 documents**: this is the essential criterion.
- **The outline is thematic** (observation / levers), not a document-by-document summary.
- **All documents are used**: verify by checking off (doc. 1: yes, doc. 2: yes, doc. 3: yes, doc. 4: yes, doc. 5: yes).
- **The research question** organizes the outline: Part I responds to the "challenge", Part II to the "competitiveness issues and solutions".
- **No personal position**: the outline presents what the documents say; it does not judge.
