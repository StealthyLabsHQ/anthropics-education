# Panel Question Bank: Thesis Defense Preparation

> Consulted when the student is preparing for their thesis defense. Provides realistic questions
> by category that the panel is likely to ask.

---

## 1. Universal questions

These questions come up in 90% of thesis defenses, regardless of the topic.

- Can you summarize your work in 2 minutes?
- What led you to choose this topic?
- If you were to do it again, what would you change?
- What is your main conclusion?
- What did you personally learn through this work?
- What is the contribution of your work? What does it bring that is new?
- What follow-up could this work lead to?

---

## 2. Questions on the research question

- Why did you formulate the research question this way?
- Is it not too broad / too narrow?
- Did you hesitate over other formulations? Which ones?
- How did you move from your initial topic to this research question?
- Does your research question actually correspond to what you ended up addressing?
  (trap: check the consistency between the stated question and the delivered work)

---

## 3. Questions on methodology

- Why did you choose this method rather than another?
- How many interviews did you conduct? Why that number?
- How did you select your interviewees / your sample?
- Did you achieve theoretical saturation? How do you know?
- How did you analyze your data? What tool did you use?
- Was your questionnaire pre-tested?
- What was the response rate of your questionnaire?
- What biases did you identify in your data collection?
- Are your results generalizable? Why / why not?
- If you had had more time, what additional methodology would you have used?

---

## 4. Questions on results

- Which result surprised you the most?
- Are there results that contradict the literature? How do you explain this?
- How do you interpret [specific result]?
- Do your results confirm or refute your initial hypothesis?
- Did you find results you did not use? Why?
- How do your recommendations follow from your results?
  (trap: check that the recommendations are grounded in the analysis, not pulled from nowhere)

---

## 5. Questions on limitations

- What are the main limitations of your work?
- Do these limitations affect the validity of your conclusions?
- How could you have worked around them?
- Is your sample representative?
- Were the conditions of your fieldwork optimal?

---

## 6. Classic trap questions

### The definition trap
"How do you define [central concept of your thesis]?"
Prepare a clear, sourced definition in 2-3 sentences. Do not stumble
over the concept that is at the heart of your own work.

### The current events trap
"Have you followed recent developments on this topic? [new law, new tool, recent publication]"
Stay informed about news related to your topic in the weeks before the defense.

### The contradiction trap
"You say on page X that... but on page Y you say that... How do you reconcile the two?"
Reread your thesis in full before the defense. Identify inconsistencies yourself.

### The "so what?" trap
"OK, you have shown that X. And concretely, what do we do with that?"
Prepare 2-3 operational, concrete, quantified recommendations if possible.

### The source trap
"You cite [author] several times. Can you tell us more about their work?"
Actually know the 3-5 most-cited authors. Have read at least their conclusions.

### The excessive humility trap
"But if your work has all these limitations, what is the point of it?"
Do not sink into self-criticism. Defend your work: "Despite these limitations,
the results show that... and open the door to..."

## 7. Questions specific by type

### Professional thesis (BUT / vocational degree / work-study master's)

- How did your host company react to your recommendations?
- Have your recommendations been implemented? If not, why?
- What was your degree of autonomy on this project?
- How did you manage the dual role of student/employee?
- How did your academic training help you in the field?
- Did your company supervisor participate in defining the topic?

### Internship report

- What did you bring to the company during this internship?
- Which task left the biggest impression on you and why?
- Did you encounter difficulties? How did you overcome them?
- Did this internship confirm or change your professional goals?
- What would you have liked to do more of during this internship?

### Research thesis

- What is your theoretical framework and why did you choose it?
- How do you position yourself relative to [author who says the opposite]?
- Does your literature review cover international work?
- Have you identified a gap in the literature? Which one?
- Could this work lead to a publication? In what form?
- Are you considering pursuing a PhD?

## 8. How to structure a response

### PREP method (30-60 seconds per response)

**P**oint: state the answer directly
**R**eason: explain why
**E**xample: illustrate with a concrete fact
**P**oint: restate the conclusion

Example:

> **Question:** "Why did you only interview 6 people?"
>
> **P:** "Six interviews seemed sufficient in this specific context."
> **R:** "The firm only has 8 staff members and 2 were on leave.
> I therefore covered almost the entire relevant workforce."
> **E:** "Moreover, from the 5th interview onward, the responses consistently
> echoed what had already been said."
> **P:** "Saturation was reached, even though an external sample
> could have enriched the perspective, which is a limitation I acknowledge."

### The 3 reflexes to have

1. **Do not panic over an unexpected question.** Take 3 seconds, rephrase
   the question out loud ("If I understand correctly, you are asking..."),
   then answer.

2. **Say "I don't know" rather than making something up.** Follow up with:
   "That is an avenue I did not explore, but one that would be worth pursuing."

3. **Do not keep justifying yourself in a loop.** Answer once, clearly, then move on.
   Repeating yourself gives the impression of not being in command of your topic.

## 9. Anti-AI trap questions (NEW)

> **Context:** An increasing number of professors use LLMs (ChatGPT,
> Claude, Gemini) themselves to generate trap questions designed to check whether the student
> actually wrote their text. The strategy is simple: ask questions that only
> someone who lived through the work can answer. A student who copy-pasted
> AI text will be unable to respond.
>
> **This file prepares the student to answer these questions, not to cheat.**
> The objective is for the student to deeply understand their own text.

### 9.1 The writing process

These questions verify that the student actually drafted the text.

| Trap question | What the professor is looking for | How to respond |
|---|---|---|
| "How did you begin writing? Which section did you start with?" | Nobody starts with the introduction. If the student says "I started at the beginning", that is suspicious. | Answer honestly. "I started with the field section because that was what I knew best. The introduction I wrote at the end." |
| "How many versions did you write of this section?" | A real writer rewrites. A copy-paster does not know. | "The introduction: I wrote three versions. The first was too long, the second too theoretical. The third is the one you read." |
| "Which passage was hardest to write?" | A real author struggled somewhere. A copy-paster struggled nowhere. | Name a specific passage and explain why: "The limitations section, because I didn't want to give the impression of demolishing my own work." |
| "Why did you phrase this sentence this way? (quotes a specific sentence)" | Testing whether the student can justify a stylistic choice. | Respond with substance: "I wanted to emphasize that this was not a deliberate choice by the company but a time constraint." |
| "Did you use writing assistance tools?" | Direct question. Lying is risky. | Be honest about tools used (spell-checker, Grammarly, paraphrasing of passages). Never deny everything if that is false. |

### 9.2 Understanding the content

These questions verify that the student understands what they wrote.

| Trap question | What the professor is looking for | How to respond |
|---|---|---|
| "Can you explain this concept (from the thesis) without looking at your notes?" | If the student copy-pasted an AI-generated theoretical passage, they will not be able to rephrase it. | Practice explaining each key concept as if talking to a friend. Not the thesis's words, your own words. |
| "You cite [author]. What do you really take away from them?" | AI cites authors without having read them. The professor knows this. | Have read at minimum the abstract, introduction, and conclusion of the 3-5 main sources. Be able to say: "What I take from this is that..." |
| "If I give you an objection to your thesis, how do you respond?" | Testing critical thinking. AI produces smooth texts without friction. | Prepare 2-3 objections yourself before the defense and have ready responses. |
| "Your thesis says X. But [contrary fact]. How do you explain it?" | The professor has checked. They raise a real fact that contradicts the thesis. | Do not panic. Either acknowledge the limitation, or explain why the thesis's case is different. |
| "What does this technical term mean exactly?" (on a word from the thesis) | If AI used a word the student doesn't master, they're caught. | Reread your thesis and verify you understand every word used. If a word is unclear, replace it before the defense or learn it. |

### 9.3 Field experience

These questions are the most formidable. They verify that the student actually
lived the experience described. An LLM cannot answer in their place.

| Trap question | What the professor is looking for | How to respond |
|---|---|---|
| "Describe a typical day during your internship/work-study." | Sensory, concrete details that only someone who was there can provide. | "I arrived at 8:30, started by checking the firm's emails, then worked on bank reconciliations until the 10 o'clock break. The afternoon was mostly data entry." |
| "What is your supervisor's name? What was their exact role?" | Verify that the student actually did the fieldwork. | Know the name, position, responsibilities. "My supervisor was Mr. Durand, he heads the payroll department, he manages the payroll for 80 clients." |
| "You mention [tool/software]. Show me how it works." | If the thesis describes a tool, the student must be able to talk about it concretely. | Prepare 2-3 screenshots or know how to describe the interface from memory. "The home screen of [software] shows the dashboard with active files on the left and alerts on the right." |
| "What went wrong that you didn't put in the thesis?" | The professor knows reality is always messier than the text. A real author has a backstory to tell. | Have 1-2 anecdotes in reserve. "Actually, in the first week we nearly lost the data because nobody had backed up before the migration." |
| "If your company called you back tomorrow to continue this project, what would you do first?" | Testing the capacity for concrete projection, not the thesis's generic recommendations. | Answer with a concrete action: "I would pick up the tracking file for the unmigrated files and call the 3 clients who had refused to switch to digital." |

### 9.4 LLM-generated questions that professors use

Professors ask LLMs: "Here is a thesis on [topic]. Generate questions
that the student will not be able to answer if they did not write the text themselves."

LLMs typically generate these categories of questions:

#### Category A: Unjustified choices in the text

> "On page 23, you choose to compare with the manufacturing sector rather than the service sector.
> Why this specific choice?"

The AI that wrote the text made this choice arbitrarily. The student who genuinely
thought it through knows why.

**Preparation:** Reread every methodological choice in the thesis and prepare a 2-sentence
justification.

#### Category B: Raw data behind the results

> "Your graph on page 31 shows a 15% increase. Can you give me the raw figures?"

AI invents consistent results but the student does not have the underlying data.

**Preparation:** Have a file (Excel, spreadsheet) with raw data
accessible during the defense. Be able to say: "Yes, it's 127,000 in year N-1 and
146,000 in year N, which is exactly 14.96% growth."

#### Category C: Alternatives that were rejected

> "You chose [tool X]. Did you test other tools? Why not [tool Y] or [tool Z]?"

AI does not know which tools were rejected or why.

**Preparation:** For each major choice (tool, method, scope), prepare
the list of alternatives considered and the reasons for the final choice.

#### Category D: Internal contradictions

> "On page 12 you say the team was reluctant. On page 34 you say adoption was rapid.
> How do you explain this contradiction?"

AI generates texts that are superficially consistent but may contradict themselves between sections.

**Preparation:** Reread your entire thesis looking for inconsistencies.
If they exist, correct them before the defense or prepare an explanation
(e.g.: "The reluctance was at the beginning; the rapid adoption covers the following 3 months").

#### Category E: "Off-script" questions

> "What if we applied your method to a 50-person firm instead of 8: would it work?"

AI cannot extrapolate beyond what it has written. A real author can
think in real time.

**Preparation:** For each conclusion, ask yourself: "Would this hold in a different context?"
Prepare 1-2 alternative scenarios.

### 9.5 Pre-defense anti-trap checklist

```
BEFORE THE DEFENSE: Mandatory checks
══════════════════════════════════════════════════

Understanding the text:
  [ ] I can explain each key concept in my own words (not the thesis's words)
  [ ] I know the 3-5 authors I cite and can speak about their work
  [ ] I understand every technical term used in my text
  [ ] I can rephrase my introduction and conclusion without reading them

Field experience:
  [ ] I can describe a typical day of my internship/work-study
  [ ] I know the name and role of my supervisor and colleagues
  [ ] I can concretely describe the tools/software mentioned
  [ ] I have 2-3 "off the record" anecdotes (what is not in the thesis)

Data and choices:
  [ ] I have access to the raw data behind my graphs and figures
  [ ] For each methodological choice, I know why I rejected the alternatives
  [ ] I know the limitations of my work and can defend them
  [ ] I have reread my thesis looking for internal contradictions

Writing process:
  [ ] I know which section I started with and why
  [ ] I can identify the passage that was hardest to write
  [ ] I am ready to state which tools I used (spell-checker, AI, etc.)
  [ ] I know what I would change if I were to do it again (concrete answer, not vague)

Projection:
  [ ] I can respond to "what if we applied this in a different context?"
  [ ] I have a concrete answer to "what would you do if you resumed this project tomorrow?"
  [ ] I can name a question my thesis does not resolve
```

### 9.6 How professors formulate their prompts to LLMs

For information, here are the types of prompts professors submit
to ChatGPT/Claude/Gemini to generate trap questions:

1. **"Here is my student's thesis. Generate 10 questions they will only be able to
   answer if they actually wrote this text."**
   → Produces categories A through E above.

2. **"Identify the passages in this thesis that seem AI-generated and propose
   questions to check whether the student understands those passages."**
   → Directly targets suspicious zones.

3. **"What inconsistencies or contradictions are there in this text?"**
   → The LLM finds flaws the student did not catch on proofreading.

4. **"If this thesis were written by a [level] student, what methodological errors
   would you expect? Does this text have them?"**
   → The professor checks whether the text is "too perfect" for the level.

5. **"Ask very concrete field questions about [the company/topic]
   that only someone who worked there could answer."**
   → Produces the field experience questions (9.3).

**The best defense against these questions: having actually done the work.**
If the student used AI as an assistance tool (rephrasing, structure, proofreading),
they must still master their topic. This skill cannot replace that mastery,
but it can help the student prepare by simulating these questions.
