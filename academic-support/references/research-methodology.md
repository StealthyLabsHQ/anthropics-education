## 1. Choosing a methodology

The choice is not random. It follows from the research question.

| Your research question looks like... | Appropriate approach |
|----------------------------------------|-----------------|
| "How do employees experience X?" | Qualitative (interviews) |
| "What is the impact of X on Y?" | Quantitative (questionnaire, data) |
| "To what extent does X influence Y in context Z?" | Mixed or quantitative |
| "What are the factors that explain X?" | Exploratory qualitative then confirmatory quantitative |
| "How does the process of X work?" | Qualitative (observation, case study) |
| "Is X correlated with Y?" | Quantitative |
| "How can X be improved?" | Action research or case study with recommendations |

### Justifying your choice

Never write "I chose the qualitative method because it is suited to my topic."
Explain WHY it is suited:

> "The phenomenon studied (the adoption of digital tools by senior accountants)
> is poorly documented in the literature. An exploratory approach using semi-structured
> interviews allows categories of analysis to emerge that existing work has not identified."

## 2. Qualitative approach

### Semi-structured interviews

**When to use:** Explore an experience, understand perceptions, gather professional practices.
This is the most common tool in professional theses and master's-level SSH research.

**Preparing the interview guide:**

```
THEME 1: [Theme name]
  Main question: ...
  Follow-up 1: ...
  Follow-up 2: ...

THEME 2: [Theme name]
  Main question: ...
  Follow-up 1: ...
  Follow-up 2: ...

[3 to 5 themes maximum]
```

Rules:
- Open questions, never closed ("How...?" not "Is...?")
- No leading questions ("Don't you think X is a problem?")
- Plan follow-ups to dig deeper without influencing
- Start with easy questions (background, role) before sensitive topics
- Ideal duration: 30-60 min per interview

**How many interviews?**

| Level | Minimum number | Saturation |
|--------|---------------|-----------|
| BUT / Vocational Bachelor's | 4-6 | When responses start repeating |
| Master's 1 | 6-10 | Same |
| Master's 2 Research | 10-15 | Formally established theoretical saturation |
| PhD | 15-30+ | Demonstrated theoretical saturation |

**Analyzing interviews: thematic method (Paillé & Mucchielli):**

1. Full transcription (verbatim)
2. Floating reading of the full set
3. Coding: identify meaning units, assign a code
4. Group codes into thematic categories
5. Build a thematic tree
6. Interpret in connection with the literature

Free tools: RQDA (R), QualCoder, or simply a spreadsheet with
columns for Verbatim / Code / Theme / Sub-theme.

### Participant observation

**When to use:** The student is on placement/work-study and observes practices
from within. Particularly suited to professional theses.

**Field journal:**
Keep a notebook (physical or digital) with:
- Date and context of the observation
- Factual description of what happened
- Impressions and interpretations (separate from the facts)
- Questions that arise

### Case study

**When to use:** Analyze a phenomenon in depth in its real context.
Methodological reference: Yin (2018).

One case = one unit of analysis (a company, a project, a department).
Multi-case = comparison of 2-4 cases to identify regularities.

Data sources in a case study:
- Internal documents
- Interviews
- Direct observation
- Numerical data

The strength of the case study is triangulation: crossing several sources
to strengthen validity.

## 3. Quantitative approach

### Questionnaire

**When to use:** Measure a phenomenon, test hypotheses, generalize
results to a population.

**Building the questionnaire:**

1. Define the variables to measure (derived from the literature review)
2. Operationalize: how each variable translates into questions
3. Types of questions:
   - Likert (1-5 or 1-7): "Strongly agree" → "Strongly disagree"
   - Multiple choice
   - Numerical scales
   - Open questions (limit to 1-2 for ease of analysis)
4. Test the questionnaire on 5-10 people before distribution (pre-test)

**Sample size:**

| Type of analysis | Recommended minimum |
|---------------|-------------------|
| Descriptive statistics (means, %) | 30 responses |
| Correlation / chi-square tests | 50-100 |
| Regression | 10-15 responses per independent variable |
| Factor analysis | 5 responses per item minimum |

For a professional thesis: 30-50 usable responses is a realistic target.
For a Master's 2 research: aim for 100+ if possible.

**Distribution tools:** Google Forms (free), LimeSurvey (open source),
Typeform, Microsoft Forms.

**Data analysis:**

| What you want to do | Tool / test |
|---------------------|-------------|
| Describe (means, distribution) | Descriptive statistics, cross-tabulations |
| Compare two groups | Student's t-test, Mann-Whitney |
| Compare 3+ groups | ANOVA, Kruskal-Wallis |
| Measure a relationship between 2 variables | Pearson or Spearman correlation |
| Test the effect of X on Y | Linear regression |
| Check the reliability of a scale | Cronbach's alpha |

Free tools: JASP, PSPP (alternative to SPSS), R, Google Sheets (for basics),
Excel with the data analysis toolpack.

### Secondary data

Use existing data: government statistical databases, Open Data, industry reports,
internal company data.

Always specify: source, date, scope, limitations.

## 4. Mixed approach

Combining qualitative and quantitative. Two classic designs:

**Exploratory sequential:**
Qualitative first (interviews to explore) → Quantitative next (questionnaire to confirm)
Use: understudied topic, need to build hypotheses before testing them.

**Explanatory sequential:**
Quantitative first (questionnaire to measure) → Qualitative next (interviews to understand results)
Use: surprising or nuanced quantitative results.

**Convergent (simultaneous):**
Qualitative AND quantitative at the same time, with cross-referencing of results.
Use: when time allows and both approaches illuminate different facets.

## 5. Writing the methodology section

### Recommended structure

```
1. Reminder of the research question and objectives
2. Epistemological positioning (Master's 2 research / PhD only)
3. Choice of approach and justification
4. Field / population / sample
   - Description and justification of the choice
   - Selection method (inclusion/exclusion criteria)
   - Size and characteristics
5. Data collection tools
   - Description of the tool (guide, questionnaire...)
   - Construction process
   - Pre-test if applicable
6. Data collection protocol
   - How, when, where data were collected
   - Practical conditions (interview duration, response rate...)
7. Analysis method
   - Software tool used
   - Step-by-step analysis procedure
8. Methodological limitations
   - Identified biases
   - What the method cannot say
```

### Epistemological positioning (Master's 2 Research / PhD)

Not required in a professional thesis or BUT. Mandatory in Master's 2 research.

| Paradigm | View | Typical method |
|-----------|--------|----------------|
| Positivist | Reality exists and is measurable | Quantitative, hypothetico-deductive |
| Interpretivist | Reality is constructed by actors | Qualitative, comprehensive |
| Constructivist | Knowledge is constructed through interaction | Mixed, action research |
| Pragmatist | What matters is what works | Mixed, results-oriented |

## 6. Common errors

| Error | Why it is a problem | Correction |
|--------|---------------------------|-----------|
| "I chose qualitative because it's simpler" | Not a methodological justification | Justify based on the nature of the research question |
| No interview guide presented | The panel cannot evaluate rigor | Put the guide in an appendix, describe it in the text |
| "I interviewed 3 people" without justification | 3 may be enough in a case study, not in broad exploration | Justify the number, mention saturation or constraints |
| Questionnaire with no pre-test | Ambiguous questions go unnoticed | Always test on 5-10 people |
| Confusing correlation with causation | "X is correlated with Y" does not mean "X causes Y" | Use appropriate vocabulary: "associated with", "linked to" |
| No mention of limitations | Gives the impression the student lacks perspective | Limitations section is mandatory, honest, and concrete |
| Methodology described in 10 lines | Most evaluated section by the panel | Develop, justify each choice |
